## Summary of the Week

The EU's AI Act suffered its first significant legislative erosion this week, as Brussels clinched a deal to roll back AI restrictions under sustained US pressure, with Germany securing a carve-out for industrial applications that marks a dramatic departure from the law's original design ([EU clinches deal to roll back AI restrictions](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-clinches-deal-to-roll-back-ai-restrictions/), [Germany set to secure win for industry in AI talks](https://www.politico.eu/article/germany-eu-ai-law-machinery-exemption-industrial-rewrite/)); industry and lawmakers immediately pushed for further deregulation, signalling that enforcement will be substantially weaker than initially legislated ([Lawmakers, industry push EU to move faster on AI rule cuts](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-ai-rule-cuts-not-enough-lawmakers-industry-more-deregulation/)). Simultaneously, the financial architecture underpinning the AI buildout came under scrutiny from multiple directions: major banks including JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley are seeking to offload credit exposure from AI data centre financing ([Building AI data centers is becoming a stress test for banks](https://the-decoder.com/building-ai-data-centers-is-becoming-a-stress-test-for-banks/)), SoftBank slashed an OpenAI-backed loan from $10 billion to $6 billion as lenders struggled to value unlisted AI assets ([SoftBank reportedly slashes OpenAI-backed loan](https://the-decoder.com/softbank-reportedly-slashes-openai-backed-loan-from-10-billion-to-6-billion-as-lenders-balk-at-private-ai-valuations/)), and the EU's own €20 billion AI computing hub initiative attracted criticism for lacking sufficient market demand to justify the allocation ([EU accused of wasting €20B on AI computing dreams](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-accused-wasting-20-billion-euro-ai-computing-dreams/)). Governance frameworks on both sides of the Atlantic showed signs of fragmentation: the White House sent contradictory signals on AI model vetting ([White House's 'lack of organization' has AI lobbyists fretting](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/05/08/trump-white-house-ai-confusion-00913092)), EU cybersecurity law was found ill-equipped to assess advanced AI threats ([Europe's laws 'ill-equipped' to deal with superhacking AI](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-europe-laws-ill-equipped-deal-with-superhacking-ai-lawmakers-warn/)), and Oxford Internet Institute warned that new EU data centre reporting rules contain loopholes that risk becoming compliance theatre ([The efficiency paradox in EU data centre policy](https://www.oii.ox.ac.uk/news-events/the-efficiency-paradox-in-eu-data-centre-policy/)). Taken together, the week's developments reveal a widening gap between the pace of AI infrastructure deployment and the capacity of regulatory and financial systems to manage the risks it generates.

## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

Followed accounts — predominantly Gary Marcus, with contributions from Yann LeCun, Sam Altman, and Andrew Ng — converged on four interlocking themes that intensified across the week.

**AI investment economics and bubble risk** was the most persistent thread. Early in the week, Marcus characterised the hyperscaler funding model as circular — cloud providers financing AI companies to purchase their own compute, masking negative free cash flow — coining the phrase "we wanted the singularity; we got the circularity" ([Mon 5 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2051804769266659488)). By mid-week, Goldman Sachs analysis cited by Marcus described AI spending dynamics as "unprecedented and unsustainable," with only semiconductor firms profiting while downstream AI companies lose money ([Thu 7 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052208998108033245)). By Friday, Morgan Stanley data showing AI infrastructure spending projected at $1.1 trillion annually — equivalent to 3.3% of GDP — prompted questions about whether investment levels are proportionate to demonstrated returns ([Fri 8 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052969463402893588)). These social discussions ran in parallel with reported news of SoftBank reducing its OpenAI-backed loan ([SoftBank reportedly slashes OpenAI-backed loan](https://the-decoder.com/softbank-reportedly-slashes-openai-backed-loan-from-10-billion-to-6-billion-as-lenders-balk-at-private-ai-valuations/)) and banks offloading data centre credit risk ([Building AI data centers is becoming a stress test for banks](https://the-decoder.com/building-ai-data-centers-is-becoming-a-stress-test-for-banks/)), suggesting that financial market participants are beginning to act on the same scepticism experts were articulating online.

**OpenAI governance and credibility** dominated the mid-week discourse. The ongoing Musk v. OpenAI trial generated sustained commentary: Marcus documented alleged conflicts of interest involving Greg Brockman's undisclosed Cerebras ownership ([Mon 5 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2051501338354475293)), and by Wednesday reported that Mira Murati's testimony indicated Sam Altman's 2023 ouster stemmed from trustworthiness concerns rather than AI safety disagreements ([Wed 6 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052077735678525820)). Marcus also noted OpenAI's violation of Canadian privacy laws in ChatGPT development ([Thu 7 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052151440924377405)), and by Friday highlighted a pattern of safety researcher departures from the organisation ([Fri 8 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052546814331736164)). These governance concerns are material to the extent that OpenAI's capital structure — including Anthropic's $200 billion Google Cloud commitment ([Anthropic commits $200 billion to Google Cloud over five years](https://the-decoder.com/anthropic-commits-200-billion-to-google-cloud-over-five-years/)) and OpenAI's own $4 billion enterprise deployment raise ([OpenAI raises over $4 billion for new enterprise deployment venture](https://the-decoder.com/openai-raises-over-4-billion-for-new-enterprise-deployment-venture/)) — rests on projections of 20–30 fold revenue growth by 2029 that remain unvalidated.

**AI regulation and the shift in government posture** evolved notably across the week. Early posts flagged EU cybersecurity gaps ([Europe's laws 'ill-equipped' to deal with superhacking AI](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-europe-laws-ill-equipped-deal-with-superhacking-ai-lawmakers-warn/)) and EU pressure on Anthropic over its Mythos model ([EU pressure builds on Anthropic over Mythos hacking risks](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-pressure-builds-on-anthropic-over-mythos-hacking-risks/)). By Thursday, Marcus noted that the White House — previously committed to a zero-regulation approach — was reconsidering, with VP Vance reportedly consulting major AI CEOs about regulatory frameworks ([Thu 7 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2052586886527369451)). However, by Friday the White House had publicly distanced itself from tighter regulation, preferring "partnership" over oversight ([White House distances itself from tighter AI regulation](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/05/07/white-house-ai-oversight-00910837)), and by Saturday the administration's "lack of organisation" was being cited by lobbyists as a source of policy uncertainty ([White House's 'lack of organization' has AI lobbyists fretting](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/05/08/trump-white-house-ai-confusion-00913092)).

**AI capability limitations and the credibility of expert claims** ran as a background theme throughout. LeCun characterised LLM-based agents as "a recipe for disaster" ([Sun 4 May social](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2051058849763737729)); Marcus challenged Jensen Huang's admission that AI "wasn't really useful until late 2025" as undermining years of infrastructure investment rationale ([Wed 6 May social](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2051833048711184550)); and by Friday, LeCun was highlighting AI's persistent limitations in learning speed relative to humans ([Fri 8 May social](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2052521584137543719)). Sam Altman provided a counterpoint, detailing technical safeguards including chain-of-thought monitoring and sandboxing for code execution ([Sat 9 May social](https://x.com/sama/status/2052853008674017288)), though these posts were framed by Marcus as insufficient given the scale of capital being deployed.

## Most Relevant Items

**1. EU AI Act rollback: deal struck, further deregulation demanded**
The EU clinched a deal this week marking the first significant delay of its AI Act, with Germany securing an exemption for industrial applications — a dramatic departure from the law's original design that would shield large swathes of manufacturing and enterprise AI from the Act's strictest requirements. Industry groups and some lawmakers immediately declared the deal insufficient, pushing for faster and deeper rule cuts, signalling that enforcement will be materially weaker than initially legislated.
