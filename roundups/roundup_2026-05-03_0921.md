## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

Several themes recurred and evolved across the two briefings, reflecting a consistent set of structural anxieties among expert commentators.

**Open source vs. proprietary AI and IP distillation** was the most persistent theme. On 1 May, [Yann LeCun](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2050177061847720371) argued that calls to ban open-source models are commercially motivated, describing distillation concerns as a "farce" and accusing major labs of "pulling the ladder" after building their own businesses on distillation. [Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049019713409814672) added sardonic commentary on "IP thieves accusing other IP thieves." By 2 May, LeCun had sharpened the framing, characterising the campaign against open-source AI as a coordinated effort by "doomers and hawks" using national security pretexts to protect proprietary American incumbents ([Yann LeCun](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2050177061847720371)). The Musk v. Altman trial gave this debate concrete institutional weight, with Musk's own admission that xAI distils OpenAI's models ([MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/01/1136800/musk-v-altman-week-1-musk-says-he-was-duped-warns-ai-could-kill-us-all-and-admits-that-xai-distills-openais-models/)).

**AI investment efficiency and economic value** was a recurring concern. On 1 May, [Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049947770966909207) amplified analysis characterising Big Tech's $700 billion annual AI spending as "the greatest capital misallocation in history." On 2 May, Marcus extended this critique to GDP measurement, arguing that AI's contribution to output reflects capital expenditure rather than genuine productivity or user value ([Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2050284439343862266)) — a concern with direct relevance to macroeconomic assessment of the AI investment cycle.

**Regulatory accountability and alignment risk** also ran through both days. On 1 May, Marcus appealed directly to Elon Musk to support AI regulation under the Trump administration, warning against leaving the sector "almost entirely unregulated" ([Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049952842010751095)), and flagged security vulnerabilities arising from AI-generated code deployed without oversight ([Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049996377678057717)). On 2 May, the concern escalated: Marcus issued a stark warning that alignment cannot be achieved through LLMs in their current form and called for resistance to large-scale deployment without robust safety solutions ([Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2050296366161940584)).

**Regulatory and media capture** emerged as a new thread on 2 May, with Marcus flagging Semafor's Silicon Valley initiative as evidence of industry influence over independent policy discourse ([Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2050207285377417591)), reinforced by the Wired investigation into the OpenAI-linked dark-money campaign ([Wired](https://www.wired.com/story/super-pac-backed-by-openai-and-palantir-is-paying-tiktok-influencers-to-fear-monger-about-china/)).

## Most Relevant Items

**1. Musk v. Altman Trial: Governance, Distillation, and Existential Risk Claims**
The first week of the Musk v. Altman trial produced significant disclosures on OpenAI's founding, corporate governance, and AI safety posture. Musk testified that he was deceived into funding OpenAI and admitted that his own company xAI distils OpenAI's models — directly undermining the distillation-as-theft narrative. Early corporate documents and email exchanges are being entered into evidence, providing rare transparency into the lab's foundational decision-making. *Sat 2 May* · [MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/01/1136800/musk-v-altman-week-1-musk-says-he-was-duped-warns-ai-could-kill-us-all-and-admits-that-xai-distills-openais-models/) · [The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/920775/evidence-exhibits-elon-musk-sam-altman-openai-trial)

**2. White House Blocks Anthropic's Mythos Expansion on Compute Grounds**
The White House has rejected Anthropic's proposal to extend access to its Mythos AI model to approximately 70 additional companies, citing concerns about compute resource limits. The decision signals an emerging U.S. policy posture of active state gatekeeping over frontier model deployment, with implications for how access to advanced AI capabilities may be rationed and governed going forward. *Thu 1 May* · [The Decoder](https://the-decoder.com/white-house-worried-about-compute-limits-as-it-blocks-wider-access-to-anthropics-mythos/)

**3. Dark-Money Campaign Frames Chinese AI as National Security Threat**
A Wired investigation found that Build American AI — a nonprofit linked to a super PAC funded by OpenAI and Andreessen Horowitz executives — is paying TikTok influencers to spread pro-AI messaging and stoke fears about Chinese AI competition. The campaign raises material concerns about corporate capture of public discourse and the integrity of the policy environment in which AI governance decisions are being made. *Sat 2 May* · [Wired](https://www.wired.com/story/super-pac-backed-by-openai-and-palantir-is-paying-tiktok-influencers-to-fear-monger-about-china/)

**4. Cybersecurity in the AI Era: Systemic Risk and Legacy Limitations**
An MIT Technology Review session from the EmTech AI conference examined how AI is expanding the attack surface and exposing the limits of legacy cybersecurity approaches. The analysis positions AI-era cyber risk as a structural challenge requiring foundational rethinking rather than incremental patching — relevant to financial sector operational resilience assessments. *Sat 2 May* · [MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/01/1136779/cyber-insecurity-in-the-ai-era/)

**5. CSET Testimony on China's Data Acquisition Strategy**
CSET Senior Fellow Andrew Lohn testified before the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission at a hearing examining China's expanding strategy for data dominance. The testimony contextualises data acquisition as a strategic instrument in AI competition, with implications for how policymakers frame data governance and cross-border information flows. *Thu 1 May* · [CSET](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/cset-senior-fellow-andrew-lohn-testifies-before-u-s-china-economic-and-security-review-commission/)

**6. Nebius Acquires US AI Inference Startup Eigen AI for $643 Million**
European AI infrastructure company Nebius has acquired Eigen AI, a US startup specialising in improving the performance of open-source AI models, in a $643 million deal. The transaction reflects ongoing consolidation in AI infrastructure and cross-Atlantic capital flows into the open-source model optimisation segment. *Sat 2 May* · [Tech.eu](https://tech.eu/2026/05/01/nebius-announces-construction-of-one-of-europes-largest-data-centres/)

**7. FDA Launches AI-Powered Clinical Trial Monitoring Pilot**
The FDA is piloting real-time AI and cloud-based monitoring of clinical trials, a move the agency says could significantly accelerate drug approval timelines. The initiative is notable as an example of a major regulatory body deploying AI to rebuild operational capacity following DOGE-related staff reductions. *Thu 1 May* · [The Decoder](https://the-decoder.com/fda-bets-on-ai-and-cloud-monitoring-for-clinical-trials-as-it-looks-to-rebuild-after-doge-layoffs/)

**8. DeepMind Alumni Drive Wave of European AI Startup Formation**
New data shows that Google DeepMind alumni have founded or are planning to found dozens of European AI startups over the past 18 months. The trend points to accelerating talent-driven ecosystem development in Europe, with potential implications for the competitive landscape and the distribution of AI capabilities outside the US. *Sat 2 May* · [Tech.eu](https://tech.eu/2026/05/01/over/)
