Europe's AI regulatory posture was exposed as structurally dependent on voluntary corporate cooperation: while the EU Commission secured access to OpenAI's GPT-5.5 Cyber model for security review, Anthropic resisted after four to five meetings, revealing that the bloc's oversight of frontier models rests on goodwill rather than binding enforcement mechanisms ([The EU wants to regulate AI but needs OpenAI and Anthropic to let regulators through the door](https://the-decoder.com/the-eu-wants-to-regulate-ai-but-needs-openai-and-anthropic-to-let-regulators-through-the-door/)). ## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

Followed accounts—dominated by Gary Marcus, with contributions from Yann LeCun, Sam Altman, Andrew Ng, and Fei-Fei Li—converged on several recurring themes across the week, with notable evolution in emphasis.

**AI hype versus reality** was the most persistent thread. Early in the week, Marcus flagged declining token consumption for major models and characterised the AI funding bubble as twice the size of outstanding subprime mortgage debt in 2008, with 40% of US retirement funds exposed to AI stocks ([Mon 11 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2053546657485299869)). By mid-week, this crystallised into concrete data: surveys predicted 43% of companies would deploy AI at scale, but the actual figure is only 19%, and ByteDance had shut down 30% of its AI projects ([Wed 13 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054291742195691987)). The debate sharpened further on Thursday, with Marcus citing Harvard Business Review research on "AI brain fry"—high performers spending more effort managing AI tools than solving original problems ([Thu 14 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054812824551981524)).

**AI safety and alignment failures** recurred throughout the week. The ChatGPT-FSU shooter lawsuit surfaced on Monday ([Mon 11 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2053690491326411014)) and was reinforced by mid-week discussion of House Oversight Committee investigations into OpenAI's governance and financial conflicts ([Tue 12 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054002904680513924)), including allegations of Sam Altman's undisclosed equity stake and a $1.4 billion personal gain from the Helion deal ([Wed 13 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054315174572466403)). By Thursday, Marcus and Yoshua Bengio's shared concern that reinforcement learning poses alignment risks through reward hacking and hidden goal formation added a technical dimension to the governance debate ([Thu 14 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054577093325779200)).

**Architectural limitations and the scaling debate** evolved from a background theme into a central dispute. LeCun and Marcus both challenged the assumption that scaling alone drives intelligence ([Mon 11 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2053992175164379596); [Fri 16 May](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2055422044477480966)), with Marcus arguing that Claude Code's integration of 53 symbolic tools validates the case for neurosymbolic architectures over pure LLMs ([Tue 12 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054015225318482075)). LeCun also highlighted research showing AI models predicting physics in latent space, suggesting models may learn representations rather than true physical understanding ([Thu 14 May](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2054750236464091627)).

**AI governance and open-source policy** became more pointed by the end of the week. Marcus noted the US had produced more AI legislation in nine months than the prior decade, yet "no clear path forward has emerged" ([Fri 16 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2055282746671399076)), while LeCun strongly defended open-source AI, comparing restrictions using "dual-use" rhetoric to failed encryption export controls ([Fri 16 May](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2055252772887429168)).

**Research and information integrity** emerged as a distinct concern from mid-week onward. Marcus flagged a 12x increase in AI-generated fabricated citations in biomedical literature since 2023 ([Tue 12 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054002904680513924)), 140,000 fake citations in 2025 alone ([Thu 15 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2054963616927912024)), and applauded arXiv's ban on unvetted AI-generated submissions ([Fri 16 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2055324956154634459)).

**AI geopolitics and sovereignty** ran as a consistent undercurrent. LeCun highlighted Project Tapestry and countered Silicon Valley dominance narratives early in the week ([Mon 11 May](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2053524170739212479)), while Anthropic's policy paper framing 2028 as a decisive US-China inflection point dominated Friday's discourse ([Fri 16 May](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2055229799954591943)).

## Most Relevant Items

### 1. EU AI Oversight Depends on Voluntary Corporate Cooperation
The EU Commission has secured voluntary access to OpenAI's GPT-5.5 Cyber model for security review, but Anthropic has resisted after four to five meetings on its Mythos model, exposing a structural enforcement gap in European AI regulation. The pattern reveals that the Commission is pursuing prescriptive oversight while lacking the technical capacity and legal leverage to compel compliance from major frontier AI developers. *(Tue 12 May)* — [The EU wants to regulate AI but needs OpenAI and Anthropic to let regulators through the door](https://the-decoder.com/the-eu-wants-to-regulate-ai-but-needs-openai-and-anthropic-to-let-regulators-through-the-door/)

### 2. AI Accelerates Vulnerability Exploitation, Obsoleting 90-Day Disclosure Window
Language models can now convert security patches into functional exploits within 30 minutes, fundamentally undermining the established 90-day vulnerability disclosure framework that underpins coordinated cybersecurity response. A veteran researcher cited in the report argues the entire disclosure process requires redesign to account for AI-accelerated attack timelines. *(Tue 12 May)* — [AI turns patches into working exploits in 30 minutes](https://the-decoder.com/ai-turns-patches-into-working-exploits-in-30-minutes-and-the-90-day-disclosure-window-is-the-casualty/)

### 3. UK AI Security Institute: AI Cyber Capabilities Doubling Faster Than Estimated
The UK's AI Security Institute has revised its estimate of how fast AI cyber capabilities are doubling—twice over—with Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview and OpenAI's GPT-5.5 now surpassing even the accelerated timeline. Mythos is the first model to clear all of AISI's cyberattack simulations; Anthropic's head of red teaming warns that "within a year, Mythos will probably look quite dumb," signalling an accelerating capability trajectory with direct implications for financial system resilience. *(Thu 15 May)* — [New Claude Mythos becomes the first AI model to clear all cyberattack simulations](https://the-decoder.com/new-claude-mythos-becomes-the-first-ai-model-to-clear-all-cyberattack-simulations-from-britains-ai-safety-institute/)

### 4. Anthropic Frames 2028 as Decisive US-China AI Inflection Point
In a policy paper timed to influence Washington policymaking, Anthropic argues that 2028 represents a now-or-never moment: either the US locks in its compute lead over China, or authoritarian governance models establish dominance over global AI development standards. The framing reflects deliberate strategic communication by a major industry actor, raising questions about how industry-shaped urgency narratives are influencing the policy agenda on AI sovereignty and compute controls. *(Fri 16 May)* — [Anthropic frames AI competition with China as a now-or-never moment for Washington](https://the-decoder.com/anthropic-frames-ai-competition-with-china-as-a-now-or-never-moment-for-washington/)

### 5. Google Detects and Stops First Known AI-Developed Zero-Day Exploit
Google's Threat Intelligence Group identified and stopped the first confirmed case of an attacker using AI to discover and weaponise a zero-day vulnerability, with a mass exploitation event targeting two-factor authentication systems averted. State-backed actors from China, North Korea, and Russia are also documented as using AI to find vulnerabilities and disguise malware, marking a qualitative escalation in the AI-enabled threat landscape. *(Wed 13 May)* — [Google says it stopped a mass cyberattack after AI was used to discover a zero-day exploit](https://the-decoder.com/google-says-it-stopped-a-mass-cyberattack-after-ai-was-used-to-discover-a-zero-day-exploit/)

The development signals a systemic escalation in AI-enabled financial crime that financial regulators and central banks must factor into operational risk and consumer protection frameworks. *(Tue 12 May)* — [Generative AI turns identity theft into an industrial-scale operation](https://the-decoder.com/generative-ai-turns-identity-theft-into-an-industrial-scale-operation/)
